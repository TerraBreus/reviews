## Embedded (Software) Engineering
This is possibly my most favorite area of interest. I love building... no ok I'll be honest. I love watching other people build these incredibly customized gadgets that look like they come from a different timeline simply because they are built to the likings of one person _(instead of the generic "sleek" appearance most big-tech companies have)_.

### Discovery Checklist
- [ ] Brainstorm/choose a mini hardware project
- [ ] Analyze 5 local vacancies for skill keywords
- [ ] Find out what a "Day in the Life" looks like
- [ ] Conclusion: What do I (not) like about it?

*   **The Mini-Project Experiment:** 
    * _Idea:_ Buy a cheap ESP32 or Arduino micro-controller. Write a small program in C that handles a physical button-press interrupt, flashes an LED, or reads temperature data. (Leverages your Codam C knowledge directly onto bare metal).
*   **What vacancies actually ask for:** 
    * _Keywords to look for:_ C/C++, FreeRTOS, Microcontrollers (ARM/AVR), I2C/SPI protocols, Oscilloscopes.
*   **Day-to-day reality:** 
    * Lots of reading datasheets (registers, memory maps), writing low-level C, and scratching your head wondering if the bug is in your code or if the physical wire is just loose.
*   **Pros & Cons:** 
    * _Pros:_ Super rewarding to see physical things move/light up because of your code. Your low-level Codam foundations give you a massive edge.
    * _Cons:_ Slower development cycles. Harder to debug than standard software.

## Cybersecurity and/or Ethical Hacking
We all have this view of the "_hackerman_" entering the matrix as 1's and 0's appear in a matrix-like stream on their monitor. But in reality, I think cybersecurity is a lot less exciting and much more in the ideology of "_If I do things a million times slightly differently, I might be able to do something unintended"_.

Still, I like the idea of knowing how security works on the interwebs. Privacy for the user and transparency for/from companies is a pretty big value of mine and I would love to put that value into practice.

### Discovery Checklist
- [ ] Complete 3 introductory labs on TryHackMe/HackTheBox
- [ ] Analyze 5 local vacancies for skill keywords
- [ ] Find out what a "Day in the Life" looks like
- [ ] Conclusion: What do I (not) like about it?

*   **The Mini-Project Experiment:** 
    * _Idea:_ Create a free account on **TryHackMe** or **Hack The Box (Academy)**. Complete the introductory paths, specifically focusing on "Binary Exploitation" or "Web Vulnerabilities". See if finding a vulnerability gives you a rush or a headache.
*   **What vacancies actually ask for:** 
    * _Keywords to look for:_ OSCP, CEH, Pen-testing, Linux, OWASP Top 10, Wireshark, Scripting (Python/Bash).
*   **Day-to-day reality:** 
    * Reading security logs, running vulnerability scanners, keeping up with the latest exploits, and writing extensive reports explaining *how* you broke something so developers can fix it.
*   **Pros & Cons:** 
    * _Pros:_ Intellectually challenging, feels like solving puzzles, aligns with personal values of user privacy.
    * _Cons:_ Massive learning curve; entry-level "Apprenticeships" can sometimes be gate-kept by heavy certifications.

## Back-End Engineering
A grey area. What does Back-End Engineering even mean? Probably something with APIs. All I know is that with projects done in the past, I often liked the execution side. The nitty-gritty system calls where usage must be clearly defined in order to work properly. 

### Discovery Checklist
- [ ] Connect WebServ to this career goal
- [ ] Analyze 5 local vacancies for skill keywords
- [ ] Find out what a "Day in the Life" looks like
- [ ] Conclusion: What do I (not) like about it?

*   **The Mini-Project Experiment:** 
    * _Idea:_ You are already building `WebServ`! That *is* an elite tier back-end project. To modern-up this stack, take the architectural concepts you learned in WebServ and rebuild a tiny REST API using a language highly sought after in Amsterdam right now (like **Go/Golang** or **Python/FastAPI**) connected to a real database (PostgreSQL).
*   **What vacancies actually ask for:** 
    * _Keywords to look for:_ REST APIs, Go/Python/Java, Docker, PostgreSQL/MySQL, AWS/Cloud, CI/CD.
*   **Day-to-day reality:** 
    * Designing database schemas, optimizing API response times, writing unit tests, and making sure different independent software services can talk to each other without crashing.
*   **Pros & Cons:** 
    * _Pros:_ Abundant job market (especially in Amsterdam). Immediate applicability of your `WebServ` network-socket knowledge.
    * _Cons:_ Can feel abstract since you don't always "see" the final visual product.
