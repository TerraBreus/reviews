### Notes and Summaries
Bites of information I wrote down and processed.

#### Day 1.
```
Call to Action.
What is it you want: I want to kickstart my career as a junior developer.
This gives readers/recruiters a reason to contact you, it is a "call to action".
```

```
Personal Branding.
The overlap of who you are and how people perceive you. Your public image.
This is what we want to emphasise when looking for a job.
They are parts that are indeed YOU (not made up) and parts that others can see (visible).

Example (not about me);
I am a social, compassionate and hard working software engineer with a growing passion for legacy migration.
```

```
The HEART Method.
A small anagram for a developed introduction touching relevant fields for recruiters.
H; How you introduce yourself.
  The opening statement. Pick some adjectives that describe you, then your current role (Software Engineer Student) and then a CTA/ambition.
E; Experience.
  Projects you have worked on that contributed to the qualities that you currently have (or make you perfect for the job).
  Focus on what you have learned.
A; Advantage.
  What do people value about working with you. What is the value you bring to the company.
R; Reason.
  Why are you applying for this specific role. Why are you interested in this specific field.
  What is your reason for applying?
T; Touch of Human.
  A small (human!!!) detail that makes you memorable.
  "I enjoy making music and hope to one day create a rock-opera."
```

```
Body Language.
It might seem obvious but you can often say things without words.
It's quite difficult to be aware of body language, especially under pressure but here are a few tips anyway:
    - Walk in with a smile.
        "Thank you so much for taking the time to interview me! I'm so excited to have this opportunity!"
    - Sit up straight.
        Slouching is a sign of disinterest.
    - Maintain open posture.
        An open posture is inviting. Keep your hands on the table (and use hand movements).
```

#### Day 2.
```
STAR Method.
People love examples that show how you've grown as an student/employee. They prove your values and make them concrete.
To break down these examples, the following structure can be used:
S - ituation.
    Explain the context. What were you doing, were you working on a group project? What was the project targeted towards?
    Be specific (though don't overdo it). Sketching the situation sets the foundation to understand the next step.
T - ask.
    What did you do? What was your task, your responsibility? Explain what role/task you were given to fulfill.
A - ction.
    How did you accomplish your task? What was your (completed) plan of action? Explain (concretely) how you completed the task set upon you.
    Avoid abstract talk like; "working my ass of" or "managing my time". Be specific: "By working every day from 14 to 18 and turning of my phone and internet so I could work uninterrupted"
R - esult.
    Show off your reepings. You just told them how and what you sowed. Now it's time to make clear what the result of your plan of action was. What have achieved in the given situation from your given task and taken plan of action.

Make sure the story flows naturally. The model can apply to "fuck ups" or group projects or personal achievements.
```

```
Try to think in both ways. They need to see whether you are a good fit for them, but also whether they are a good fit for you.
A few questions you can ask yourself to find out whether the company matches;
```

```
If you are unsure whether the position is really for you, you don't have to state that. Focus on why you DO think it is right.
```
